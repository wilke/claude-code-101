# 2026-06-24 — The independent-Poisson goal model (a dead end)

Built the goal model that turns ratings into scorelines, so we can report win/draw/loss
probabilities instead of a bare pick. First attempt: **independent Poisson**.

- **Approach.** Model home goals ~ Poisson(λ_home) and away goals ~ Poisson(λ_away),
  with λ from the rating gap (+ home term when not neutral). Assume the two scores are
  **independent**, so P(scoreline) = P(home goals) · P(away goals).
- **Dead end.** Independent Poisson **overstates low-score draws** — it systematically
  puts too much probability on 0–0 and 1–1. Checked against the historical results in
  `matches.csv`: observed 0–0 and 1–1 rates were noticeably below what the independent
  model predicted. The reason is real: in football the two teams' scores are **not**
  independent at low scores (a 0–0 is its own tactical state), and independent Poisson
  can't represent that.
- **Fix (supersedes the above).** Apply the **Dixon–Coles correction**: a low-score
  dependence parameter `ρ` that down-weights 0–0/1–1 and re-balances toward 1–0/0–1.
  With `ρ` fit alongside the rate parameters, the predicted low-score distribution
  matched the observed one much better.
- **Parameter.** Dixon–Coles `ρ` ≈ −0.05 (small, negative, as expected); rate
  parameters otherwise unchanged.

**Decision.** Independent Poisson is abandoned as the goal model; the Dixon–Coles
variant replaces it. Keep the independent version only as the pedagogical "before"
picture — it's the cleanest illustration of *why* the correction exists.

**Open question:** is Poisson+Dixon–Coles enough, or do we need a negative-binomial to
handle over-dispersion in high-scoring blowouts? Deferred — the low-score fix was the
urgent one.
