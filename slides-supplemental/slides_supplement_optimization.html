Write your first CLAUDE.md

Your first contact with file-based collaboration: encode your conventions for a small optimization in one markdown file, then watch the assistant respect them the second time.

Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 1 · framing

Why an optimization researcher needs CLAUDE.md

Optimization research is full of recurring micro-decisions — small, stable choices that shape every result. CLAUDE.md is where they live, so you don't re-justify them every prompt.

DecisionWhy it belongs in CLAUDE.md
Variable naming (primal, dual, multipliers)Pinned by the math. Letting the assistant pick its own letters mid-project breaks readability across files and notes.
Solver choice and modeling stackPyomo vs CasADi vs raw scipy.optimize. IPOPT vs BARON vs HiGHS. Each comes with assumptions about problem shape; switching silently is silently wrong.
Tolerances and terminationOne number with outsized effect on what counts as "converged". Easy to set wrong, easy to change without noticing.
Figure conventions for convergence plotsSemilog axes, ‖∇f‖ vs iteration, file format, output directory. Three of these wrong and the figure is unusable in a paper.
Sanity-check valuesFinal gradient norm, function value at optimum, KKT residual. Eyeball-able numbers that distinguish "solved" from "lucky".

CLAUDE.md is read at the start of every session. The assistant doesn't re-derive your conventions — it inherits them.

Exercise 1 15 min

Let /init discover your conventions

  1. Open the exercise folder: cd exercises-opt/01-claude-md/
  2. Read max_conopt.py — a deliberately mysterious constrained optimization (the COPS 3.0 max-area polygon, solved with unopy + multistart). It has no plot, and its docs describe the interface but omit what the variables mean geometrically.
  3. Phase 1, with no CLAUDE.md. Run claude and ask: add a conopt solution plot and a convergence plot to max_conopt.py. Watch for tells that Claude doesn't know the coordinates are polar: does it plot u, v as raw x/y? do axes run to ~π? is the polygon left open?
  4. Phase 2. Download the COPS 3.0 PDF, /clear, then run /init. Read the generated CLAUDE.md — did it deduce the polar coordinate system and the conversion formula straight from the code?
  5. Re-ask the exact same prompt and compare Phase 1 vs Phase 2.
Discussion: which formula let /init infer the coordinate system? And what would you still have to add by hand that /init can't infer — figure conventions, solver output, house style?

Exercise 1 · debrief

What /init changed between the two tries

The payoff is the contrast. With only the thin docs, Claude guesses; after /init reads the source, it inherits the geometry. Use this as a checklist, not an answer key.

SignalPhase 1 (no context) → Phase 2 (after /init)
Coordinate systemPlots u, v as raw x/y → converts polar (r, θ) to Cartesian, so the shape reads as an actual polygon.
Closed polygonLast vertex left dangling → polygon closed, with the fixed final vertex placed where the problem pins it.
Convergence plotGeneric loss-vs-iteration → the multistart staircase, best objective improving in steps across restarts.
What /init inferredThe polar meaning of the variables, deduced from what the area objective and the diameter constraint have in common.
What /init could not inferFigure paths and format, solver-output preferences, house style — those you still add to CLAUDE.md by hand.

The lesson mirrors the FEM track's: /init recovers the math that lives in the code; conventions and safety rules are always on you.

Exercise 2 · plan mode

Plan a small NLP before writing it

Here the deliverable is the plan, not code. Plan mode is the method; the plan.md you'd be comfortable approving is the artifact. Fifteen minutes deciding what to pin before any compute is spent.

Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 2 · framing

Why a plan first for a from-scratch model

Building a new model is not like fixing a known one — there is no textbook answer to recite. Instead the work hides dozens of small modeling decisions, and plan mode is what forces them into the open before any get silently baked into code.

DecisionWhat plan mode forces you to settle in advance
Covariance handlingHow Σ is loaded and validated PSD. A silent eigenvalue clip or ridge changes the problem — and your reported risk.
ParameterizationWhether τ and K are arguments or hardcoded. Sweeping τ is the whole exercise; a baked-in value can't move.
Complementarity constraintHow wᵀ(1−z) ≤ 0 is enforced. A big-M, smoothing, or top-K substitution turns the cardinality model into a different, easier one.
Benchmark schemaThe CSV columns and path — one row per τ, named fields, configurable location — decided before, not during.
Solver & gap definitionWhich UNO solver, and what "optimality gap" means for a nonconvex MPCC. Unstated, the recorded numbers aren't comparable.
Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 2 · principle

Claude is a tool. You are the scientist.

On a greenfield model there is no rehearsed answer for the assistant to recall — but that does not make it safe. It will quietly pick a default for every modeling decision the prompt leaves open: how to enforce complementarity, which solver to call, how to condition the covariance, what "converged" means. Any of those defaults can turn the problem you posed into a different, easier one.

Your job in plan mode is to decide which decisions you must own and pin them in the prompt or during iteration — the "keep the model exactly as formulated; flag, don't silently reformulate" clause is one example. When you catch yourself imposing the same structure every session, promote it into your CLAUDE.md.

The danger isn't that Claude flounders — it's that a silent modeling substitution looks exactly like a correct plan until you check. The check has to come from you.

Exercise 2 15 min

Run the exercise

  1. cd exercises-opt/02-planning && claude. The problem: sparse mean-variance portfolio selection with a cardinality constraint, modeled as complementarity (data in nlp_seed.py).
  2. Press Shift+Tab twice to enter plan mode (look for the indicator at the bottom of the screen).
  3. Paste the prompt and submit. Do not approve yet — it asks for a unopy formulation, a Pareto sweep over τ, a benchmarking harness to CSV, and a risk/return plot, and it carries a scope clause: 
    … Keep the model exactly as formulated in nlp_seed.py: do not
relax, smooth, or reformulate the complementarity constraint, and
do not switch solver families. If a reformulation is necessary,
flag it in the plan rather than adopting it silently.
  4. Read the plan against the checklist: Σ validated PSD? τ/K parameterized? complementarity kept intact? CSV schema + path? solver & gap defined? Push back on at least one.
  5. Save it: ask Claude to write the plan to plan.md. That file is the deliverable.

Exercise 2 · debrief

What a plan worth approving contains

A plan that survives review on this exercise pins each decision the prompt left open, and bounds its own scope. Read it against this checklist.

Look forWhy it matters
Σ loaded and validated PSDA non-PSD covariance silently changes the problem.
τ and K parameterizedThe Pareto sweep depends on it; hardcoded values can't move.
Complementarity enforced as writtenWatch for a silent relaxation that makes the model easier.
CSV path + schema fixedOutput you can't relocate, or whose columns drift, is unusable across runs.
Scope boundedA good plan names what it isn't doing, so scope doesn't creep during implementation.

Exercise 2 · closing

What plan mode is really for

Plan mode produces text. The value isn't the text — it's the modeling questions the act of planning forces you to answer while redirection is still cheap. On a from-scratch model those are exactly the questions Claude would otherwise answer for you, silently, with a plausible default. Approve the plan only once it is your model on the page.

Take the conventions you found yourself imposing back to your CLAUDE.md — the next exercise builds on that file again.

Exercise 3 · skills

Verify a KKT point with a skill

Skills are the workshop's mechanism for packaging the check your team runs every project. The check is the pretext; the skill is the artifact.

Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 3 · framing

Why a skill (and not just a function)?

A skill is a folder Claude loads on demand — SKILL.md plus helper scripts. The shape is simple; what makes it earn its keep is four properties that a private function or a CLAUDE.md note do not have.

PropertyWhy it earns the skill its keep
Triggered by description, not importClaude routes a natural-language prompt to the skill via the description: field — you do not tell it which file to run.
Stateless and deterministicSame input gives the same output. No external service to be down, no license dance.
Versioned in the repoThe check travels with the project. git clone brings the team's KKT check along, no per-developer setup.
The description is the contractExtend the helper and the description must change too. An extension that does not change the description is invisible to future natural-language requests.
Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 3 15 min

Run the exercise

  1. cd exercises-opt/03-skills
  2. Install the skills into the project-local hidden directory: 
    mkdir -p .claude
cp -R skills .claude/
  3. Launch claude. The helper is pure Python + NumPy — no container needed for this exercise.
  4. Paste the prompt — note that it does not name the skill; description-routing is what is being tested: 
    Verify the candidate solution in solution.json against the QP
defined in problem.py.
  5. Approve the shell call when Claude prompts you (default permission policy flags new commands). Read the PASS output — all five KKT residuals near machine precision.
  6. Edit solution.json: change z[0] from 0.0 to 0.5. Re-ask. Confirm FAIL with non-zero stationarity and complementarity residuals.
  7. Restore. Re-run. Back to PASS.
Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 3 · debrief

What you should see

Read Claude's behavior as much as the skill's output. The pass / fail / restore loop is deterministic; what varies between learners is how cleanly the skill mechanism worked end-to-end.

What you should seeDetail
Passing runAll five residuals at or near machine precision: stationarity ~ 1e-17, eq feas = 0, min x_i > 0, min z_i >= 0, complementarity = 0. Verdict PASS.
Failing runAfter corrupting z[0]: stationarity jumps to ~5e-1, complementarity to ~7e-2. The two residuals that flip localize the defect to the dual block.
What flippedThe lead (PASS → FAIL) and exactly the residuals that depend on z. The other three are unchanged — that is the whole point of the per-block layout.
Behavior to watchDid Claude run the skill, or recompute the KKT residuals inline? Did it show the full output, or paraphrase? Did it second-guess the verdict?

Exercise 3 · closing

Skills as your team's checklists

Every research group has a small set of checks they re-derive every project — KKT residuals, CFL bounds, convergence-rate fits, mesh-quality reports, citation-format validators. Skills are the place those checks earn back their authorship cost. The price is small (a SKILL.md and a script); the payoff is that everyone in the group, and every future Claude session, runs the same check the same way.

If you only remember one rule about skills: the description is the contract. When you extend a skill, update its description in the same commit — otherwise the new capability is invisible to natural-language routing, and the next person to ask for it will silently re-derive what you already built.

Exercise 4 · LOGBOOK.md

Bootstrap a LOGBOOK.md from project plans

LOGBOOK.md is how this session leaves something behind for the next one. The synthesis is the pretext; the file is the artifact.

Optimization supplement · v1

Exercise 4 · framing

Why a LOGBOOK.md (and not just more CLAUDE.md)?

Both are markdown files at the project root, both read by Claude on session start. They answer different questions, and confusing the two is the most common decay path for both.

QuestionAnswer
What does CLAUDE.md answer?How does this project work. Stack, file layout, conventions, hard rules.
What does LOGBOOK.md answer?What have we learned. Decisions, parameters, dead ends, open questions.
Growth patternCLAUDE.md changes rarely. LOGBOOK.md grows as experiments accumulate.
The testIf knowing a fact depends on having run experiments, it belongs in LOGBOOK.md. Otherwise CLAUDE.md.

Exercise 4 10 min

Run the exercise

  1. cd exercises-opt/04-memory — a real project's plans/ directory is already there (an ADMM filter method for topology optimization), alongside its src/ and tex/.
  2. Launch claude and paste: 
    read everything under plans/ and produce LOGBOOK.md with the
sections: Decisions, Parameters, Dead Ends, Open Questions.
Each entry should cite the plans/ file it came from.
  3. Trim aggressively. For each line ask: would a future-me on another session actually consult this? If the file is longer than the source plans combined, push back — that is copying, not synthesis.
  4. With the trimmed file (and a CLAUDE.md) in place, paste: 
    given LOGBOOK.md and CLAUDE.md, what would be the most informative
next experiment to run? Justify in two sentences.
    A good answer cites a specific entry; a hand-wave is a teachable failure.
  5. Close with the two-minute ritual: 
    summarize what we did this session, append a dated entry to
LOGBOOK.md, and overwrite STATUS.md with where we are now.

Exercise 4 · debrief

What a good synthesis looks like

The exercise tests editorial judgment more than computational behavior. Read the synthesized file and Claude's next-experiment answer against this checklist.

What a good synthesis doesWhat to push back on
Cites the source plans/ file on every entry."See plans" or no citations at all.
Comes in well under the combined length of the source plans.Longer than the inputs — re-typing, not synthesis.
Dead Ends preserve the why a path was abandoned.Verdict only ("abandoned X"). The reason is what stops the re-attempt later.
Next-experiment answer names a specific entry and a one-session scope.Generic "more research needed," or a multi-week project that ignores LOGBOOK.md.
Surfaces conflicts between two plans rather than smoothing them over.Silent merge of entries that disagree — destroys the most valuable information.

Exercise 4 · closing

LOGBOOK.md as next-session inheritance

Every session is downstream of a hundred forgotten ones — your own from last month, your colleagues', the one you'll resume on a different machine in three weeks. LOGBOOK.md is the file that decides what the next session knows. Without it, every resumption pays the same "what was I working on" tax.

The two-minute end-of-session ritual is the workshop's most lopsided ROI move: append a dated entry to LOGBOOK.md, overwrite STATUS.md with where we are now. Two minutes now; saves the first ten minutes of the next session, every time.
